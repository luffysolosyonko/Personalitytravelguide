GERMANY

Germany, country of north-central Europe, traversing the continent’s main physical divisions, from the outer ranges of the Alps northward across the varied landscape of the Central German Uplands and then across the North German Plain. One of Europe’s largest countries, Germany encompasses a wide variety of landscapes: the tall, sheer mountains of the south; the sandy, rolling plains of the north; the forested hills of the urbanized west; and the plains of the agricultural east.

Germany is home to over 80 million people – as well as a diverse array of religions, customs, and traditions that make up the rich national psyche. Germany is known for its long and rich history, one that has put it at the forefront of European thought, politics, and art for over 1,000 years. This history has shaped a culture that combines predominantly Christian values with literature, art, philosophy, logic, reason, and, of course, a love of beer and sausages.

Part of a culture that values planning and formalities, Germans are generally reserved. Punctuality is greatly prized. Rules are extremely important to Germans. Do not jaywalk or litter, as you may be subject to a fine. Although many people speak English in large cities like Munich, it is helpful to learn a few German phrases. Remember to say bitte (“please” and “you're welcome”), as well as danke (“thank you”), and you'll make a good impression. You should also be mindful of your mannerisms. Whistling at the end of or during a performance is considered a sign of displeasure. When interacting with people one-on-one, remember that touching during conversation or standing less than an arm's length apart on the first meeting is considered an invasion of privacy. Germany's official currency is the euro. Since the euro to U.S. dollar exchange rate fluctuates, be sure to check what the current exchange rate is before you go. Major credit cards are accepted at some restaurants and shops, but cash is still king in Germany, so be sure to always have some with you.

Berlin

Berlin, the capital city of Germany, is renowned for its exceptional range of landmarks, vibrant cultural scene and way of life that's somehow all go yet relaxed.
Unquestionably one of the coolest cities in the world, Berlin has a lot more to offer than just beer and bratwurst. The German capital’s famed art scene, coupled with an indefatigable nightlife, gives the city its energetic yet unequivocally gritty character. Described by a former mayor as “poor and sexy”, Berlin attracts people from all over the world with its air of cool mystery, creating a diverse population of over 3.6 million people.

Brandenburg Gate (Brandenburger Tor)
Inspired by the Acropolis entrance in Athens, the Brandenburger Tor (Brandenburg Gate) is one of the most-photographed sites in Berlin. Located in Pariser Platz (Paris Square), one of the city's most famous squares, the Brandenburg Gate was built for King Frederick William II starting in 1788. Designed by royal architect, Carl Gotthard Langhans, the sandstone structure stands 85 feet high.

Since its debut, it's been the backdrop of much of the city's history, including Napoleonic invasions and Nazi parades. During the Cold War, the structure sat in "no man's land" between East and West Berlin, where it was inaccessible to locals and visitors.

Recent visitors said it's a must-see sight that you’ll likely pass by several times during your time in Berlin thanks to its central location. Others recommended admiring it during the day and again at night, when the structure is beautifully illuminated. Although it will only take a few moments to snap a picture of the gate, you might want to walk by the tourist information center in the south wing of the Brandenburg Gate and consider the vast history that has taken place here. The gate is accessible 24/7 and is free to visit.

MUSEUMSINSEL
Museumsinsel (or Museum Island) is the name given to a group of five museums, the Baroque-style Berlin Cathedral and large gardens clustered on a tiny island in the River Spree. Built between 1823 and 1930, some museums present a different aspect of German history and art, while others spotlight global arts and antiques, such as the Egyptian Museum and Pergamon Museum.

Recent travelers said that even if you don't have time to explore all of the museums, the architecture of the buildings and the well-managed gardens are perfect for a stroll or outdoor picnic. If you'd like to visit any of the museums, reviewers suggested purchasing your tickets in advance.

MUNICH

Visitors flock to Munich every fall to take part in Oktoberfest, a two-week-long ode to Bavarian traditions featuring Germany's best beers and bratwurst. But Munich, one of the largest German cities, offers so much more than just this festival.

This energetic area is a hub for shopping, dining, and entertainment, featuring trendy boutiques, cafes, and the iconic Hachiko Statue.

Munich is just a short distance from the Bavarian Alps, making it a great base for day trips to ski resorts, scenic lakes, and charming mountain towns.

MUNICH
Munich, the vibrant capital of Bavaria, is a city where tradition meets modernity. Known for its picturesque architecture, world-renowned beer culture, and the annual Oktoberfest celebration, Munich is a captivating blend of history, art, and innovation. Visitors can explore grand palaces like Nymphenburg and the Residenz, stroll through lush urban parks such as the Englischer Garten, and immerse themselves in Bavarian culture at iconic beer halls like the Hofbräuhaus.

Beyond its historic roots, Munich is also a modern metropolis with a thriving tech and automotive scene, exemplified by the BMW Museum and BMW Welt. The city's dynamic neighborhoods, like the artsy Schwabing and historic Altstadt (Old Town), offer an eclectic mix of local flavors, shops, and entertainment. Cultural landmarks like the Deutsches Museum, Marienplatz with its iconic Glockenspiel, and the majestic Frauenkirche cathedral showcase Munich’s rich heritage, while nearby excursions to the Alps make it a haven for nature lovers.

Whether you're savoring traditional Bavarian dishes, exploring world-class museums, or simply relaxing in one of the city’s famous beer gardens, Munich offers an unforgettable experience, blending old-world charm with modern sophistication.

MARIENPLATZ
Marienplatz is the vibrant heart of Munich and a must-see destination for visitors. This historic square, dating back to the 12th century, is home to some of the city’s most iconic landmarks, including the stunning Neues Rathaus (New Town Hall) with its famous Glockenspiel. Every day, crowds gather to watch this unique clock tower come to life, with life-sized figures performing scenes from Bavarian history. Surrounded by charming shops, cafes, and restaurants, Marienplatz offers the perfect blend of cultural heritage and modern energy. From here, you can easily explore nearby attractions like the Frauenkirche and Viktualienmarkt, making Marienplatz an essential starting point for any Munich adventure.

Recent travelers say spending some time in this city square is a must-do, noting the distinctly Bavarian feel. Past visitors also recommended timing your visit for the daily glockenspiel shows at the New Town Hall (Neues Rathaus), which take place at 11 a.m. and noon. The square is free to visit 24/7, though individual businesses operate their own hours.

Nymphenburg Palace (Schloss Nymphenburg)
This grand baroque palace was the summer residence of the former rulers of Bavaria and is a must-see for its opulent architecture and vast, beautifully landscaped gardens.

The Nymphenburg Palace boasts ornately decorated rooms, including the famous Hall of Mirrors and the Gallery of Beauties. The expansive grounds include lakes, fountains, and smaller pavilions that offer peaceful walks and stunning views.

ENGLISCHER GARTEN
The Englischer Garten (English Garden) is one of the world’s largest urban parks, larger even than New York’s Central Park, and a peaceful oasis in the heart of Munich. This sprawling green space offers a tranquil escape with its scenic meadows, winding paths, and serene lakes. The park is famous for its Eisbach River, where you can watch surfers tackle the unique standing wave, and its traditional beer gardens, where locals and visitors alike gather to enjoy a refreshing drink. Whether you're in the mood for a leisurely walk, a bike ride, or a relaxing picnic, the Englischer Garten provides an idyllic setting for unwinding and taking in Munich’s natural beauty.

Recent travelers enjoyed their time here and were impressed by the park’s size, which sprawls across nearly 1,000 acres (making it one of the world’s largest inner-city parks). Many recommend renting a bike or a rowboat to make the most of your time here. Visitors said the park offered a peaceful retreat from Munich’s urban setting.

The English Garden is easily reached on the U3 and U6 U-Bahn lines along the Giselastraße, Münchner Freiheit or Universität stops. Admission is free.

Germany offers a travel experience like no other, where history, culture, and innovation come together in perfect harmony. From the lively beer halls of Munich and the artistic neighborhoods of Berlin to the romantic castles along the Rhine and the scenic peaks of the Bavarian Alps, Germany is a country that invites exploration and discovery at every turn. Whether you're savoring hearty regional cuisine, visiting world-class museums, or simply strolling through charming cobblestone streets, each city and village holds its own unique charm and story.

As you journey through Germany, you'll find yourself immersed in a rich tapestry of past and present, where medieval architecture stands alongside cutting-edge design and where tradition lives on in festivals, crafts, and local customs. The welcoming spirit, stunning landscapes, and vibrant urban life make Germany a place that leaves a lasting impression on all who visit. A trip here is more than just a vacation—it’s a journey into the heart of Europe, filled with unforgettable moments and timeless memories.
